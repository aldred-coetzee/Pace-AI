
# Injury Prevention Knowledge Base for AI Running Coach

## 1. Common Running Injuries

### Overall Epidemiology

- Annual injury incidence among runners: **37-51%**, with 2.5-12.1 injuries per 1,000 hours of running ([Systematic Review, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8500811/))
- **50-75%** of all running injuries are overuse injuries from constant repetition ([PMC Systematic Review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8500811/))
- Most common injury location: **knee (24.3%)**, followed by calf/lower leg and hip/pelvis ([Physical Activity and Health Journal](https://paahjournal.com/articles/10.5334/paah.109))
- Recurrence rate: **20-70%** of running injuries recur ([JOSPT Prospective Cohort Study](https://www.jospt.org/doi/10.2519/jospt.2021.9673))

### 1.1 Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome (Runner's Knee / PFPS)

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Prevalence** | 15-30% of female runners, 13-25% of male runners |
| **Causes** | Abnormal patellar tracking; weak quadriceps and glutes; tight hamstrings; hip abductor weakness causing knee valgus; training errors (60-80% of cases); rapid increases in mileage/speed/hills |
| **Risk factors** | Flat feet or high arches; quadriceps weakness; hip abductor/external rotator weakness; overstriding; sudden training changes |
| **Early warning signs** | Diffuse anterior knee pain worsening with stairs, squatting, or prolonged sitting ("movie theater sign"); pain after running that improves with rest; crepitus/clicking |
| **Recovery timeline** | 4-8 weeks with conservative treatment; high recurrence risk without strength work |
| **Key evidence** | 2018 International Patellofemoral Research Retreat: exercise therapy (hip AND knee focused) is first-line treatment ([PMC Evidence Review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6829001/)) |

### 1.2 Iliotibial Band Syndrome (ITBS)

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Prevalence** | 5-14% of runners; most common cause of lateral knee pain in runners |
| **Causes** | Repetitive friction/compression of IT band at lateral femoral condyle; weak hip abductors; poor biomechanics; running on banked surfaces; overstriding |
| **Risk factors** | Hip rotation weakness; preexisting IT band tightness; running on cambered roads; increased training volume without adaptation |
| **Early warning signs** | Sharp or burning pain on outer knee, typically starting 10-15 minutes into a run; pain worsens downhill; may resolve when stopping but returns earlier in subsequent runs |
| **Recovery timeline** | 2 weeks to 5 months; most recover within 6 weeks with conservative treatment; 44% complete cure rate at 8 weeks, 91.7% at 6 months ([PMC Review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4590904/)) |
| **Key evidence** | Increasing cadence by 5-10% reduces overstriding and IT band loading; hip strengthening is primary treatment |

### 1.3 Medial Tibial Stress Syndrome (Shin Splints / MTSS)

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Prevalence** | 13.6-20% of runners; up to 35% in military recruits |
| **Causes** | Tibial bony overload with periostitis; training load errors (too much too soon); bone adapts slower than muscles/cardiovascular fitness |
| **Risk factors** | Female sex; higher BMI; flat feet (overpronation); prior history of MTSS; sudden increases in distance/speed/hills; running on hard surfaces |
| **Early warning signs** | Diffuse pain along medial tibia during/after exercise; tenderness over >5cm of tibial border (distinguishes from stress fracture); pain that initially warms up but progressively worsens |
| **Recovery timeline** | 2-6 weeks for mild cases; 3-6 months for chronic cases; if untreated, can progress to tibial stress fracture |
| **Key evidence** | Gold standard is graded loading, not complete rest; calf strengthening is critical; optimize calcium/vitamin D status ([PMC Shin Splint Review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9937638/), [StatPearls MTSS](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK538479/)) |

### 1.4 Plantar Fasciitis

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Prevalence** | 4.5-10% of runners; third most common running injury |
| **Causes** | Degenerative pathology (not purely inflammatory); overuse of plantar fascia at calcaneal insertion; tight calves/Achilles reducing dorsiflexion; poor footwear |
| **Risk factors** | Limited ankle dorsiflexion; high BMI; excessive pronation; tight calf muscles; high weekly mileage; standing occupations |
| **Early warning signs** | "First step pain" -- sharp heel pain with first steps in morning or after sitting; pain eases with movement but returns after prolonged activity; tenderness at medial heel |
| **Recovery timeline** | Mild cases: a few weeks; typical: 6-12 months for full resolution; 90-95% respond to conservative treatment ([StatPearls Plantar Fasciitis](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK431073/), [PMC Athlete Review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5505577/)) |
| **Key evidence** | Graded loading (heel raises), stretching, and orthoses are evidence-based; plantar fascia and Achilles tendon are part of the same kinetic system |

### 1.5 Achilles Tendinopathy

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Prevalence** | 9.1-10.9% of runners |
| **Causes** | Repetitive overload exceeding tendon capacity; poor load management; calf weakness; limited dorsiflexion |
| **Risk factors** | Rapid training increases; hill running; age >35; male sex; previous Achilles injury; stiff/unsupportive footwear |
| **Early warning signs** | Stiffness/pain in Achilles region first thing in morning; mild ache after running that progresses to pain during running; palpable thickening of tendon |
| **Two types** | Insertional (at heel bone, easier to treat) vs. mid-portion (2-6cm above insertion, more common in runners) |
| **Recovery timeline** | 3-6 months typical; chronic cases may take 12+ months |
| **Key evidence** | Eccentric heel-lowering exercises (Alfredson protocol: 3x15 reps, 2x/day) are first-line treatment for mid-portion; heavy slow resistance loading also effective ([AAFP Common Running Injuries](https://www.aafp.org/pubs/afp/issues/2018/0415/p510.html)) |

### 1.6 Stress Fractures

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Prevalence** | 1-20% of athletic injuries; ~16% of all running injuries; annual incidence possibly >20% in runners |
| **Most common sites** | Tibia (23.6%), tarsal navicular (17.6%), metatarsals (16.2%), femur (6.6%), pelvis (1.6%) |
| **Causes** | Rate of microfracture exceeds rate of bone repair; training errors (30% occur within 12 weeks of training change); relative energy deficiency (RED-S) |
| **Risk factors** | Previous stress fracture (strongest); female sex; low BMI (<19); vitamin D deficiency; insufficient calcium; low energy availability (RED-S); menstrual dysfunction; rapid training progression |
| **Early warning signs** | Localized bone pain that worsens with impact and improves with rest; night pain; pain that progressively starts earlier in runs; point tenderness over bone |
| **Low-risk sites** | Tibial shaft, fibula, metatarsals 2-4: heal well conservatively in 6-12 weeks |
| **High-risk sites** | Femoral neck, anterior tibia, navicular, 5th metatarsal, sesamoids: require more aggressive management, possibly surgery, 3-6 months recovery |
| **Key evidence** | Imaging confirmation recommended; return-to-run only after pain-free walking; require radiological healing confirmation for high-risk sites; optimize calcium (1200mg/day), vitamin D, and energy availability ([PMC BSI Management](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4384749/), [JOSPT BSI Prevention](https://www.jospt.org/doi/10.2519/jospt.2014.5334)) |

### 1.7 Hamstring Injuries

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Mechanism** | Occur during late swing phase when hamstrings produce large eccentric forces at long lengths to decelerate the leg |
| **Causes** | Insufficient eccentric strength; short biceps femoris fascicles (<10.56cm = 4.1x risk); neuromuscular control deficits; lumbar-pelvic dysfunction; anterior pelvic tilt |
| **Risk factors** | Previous hamstring injury (strongest); age; fatigue; poor eccentric strength; inadequate warm-up |
| **Early warning signs** | Tightness in posterior thigh that worsens during speed work; localized tenderness; pain with hip flexion/knee extension |
| **Recovery timeline** | Grade 1: 1-3 weeks; Grade 2: 4-8 weeks; Grade 3: 3-6 months; re-injury rates are high |
| **Key evidence** | Nordic hamstring exercises reduce hamstring injuries by **~51%** (systematic review of 8,459 athletes); L-protocol (eccentric lengthening) reduces re-injury risk vs. conventional rehab and enables faster return to play; compliance is critical (91% compliance = benefit, 21% compliance = no benefit) ([PMC Nordic Hamstring Review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11311354/), [PubMed Meta-Analysis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30808663/)) |

## 2. Strength Training for Runners

### 2.1 Evidence Summary

- A supervised hip and core exercise program reduced lower-extremity overuse injuries by **39%** and substantial overuse injuries by **52%** in novice runners (Leppanen et al., 2024, Run RCT, published in *British Journal of Sports Medicine*) ([PubMed Run RCT](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38724071/))
- Unsupervised strength programs show minimal benefit: NYC Marathon RCT found 0% injury reduction with self-directed 10-minute program 3x/week ([PubMed NYC Marathon Study](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31642726/))
- Supervision and compliance are the strongest moderators of effectiveness ([PMC Scoping Review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11986186/))
- Multi-component programs (strength + balance + coordination) are more effective for ankle and knee injuries than single-component strength alone ([PMC Meta-Analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12099121/))

### 2.2 Frequency and Loading Parameters

| Parameter | Recommendation | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Frequency** | 2-3 sessions per week | Systematic reviews support 2-3x/week for 8-12 weeks ([PubMed Balsalobre-Fernandez](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26694507/)) |
| **Intensity** | Heavy: >=70% 1RM (4-10 reps) | Heavy resistance training improves running economy more than bodyweight (g = -0.32) ([PMC Heavy vs Plyometric](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9653533/)) |
| **Volume** | 2-4 lower-body exercises per session | Low to moderate volume is sufficient |
| **Timing** | Before running or on separate days | Run RCT performed strength exercises before running sessions |
| **Duration** | 20-35 minutes per session | Run RCT protocol: 8 exercises in 20-35 minutes |
| **Minimum effective dose** | 1-2 sessions/week can still be effective | For maintenance; 2-3 for injury prevention |

### 2.3 Why Heavy Resistance Over Bodyweight

- Heavy strength training achieves both muscular strength AND tendon stiffness ("springiness"), while light/high-rep training achieves muscular strength but NOT tendon stiffness
- Tendons, not muscles, are often the tissue that fails in runners
- Explosive/heavy training leads to neural adaptations rather than hypertrophy, so runners do not gain unwanted weight
- Plyometric training is more effective at lower speeds (<=12 km/h), while heavy resistance is more effective at higher speeds and in well-trained athletes ([PMC Running Economy Meta-Analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11052887/))

### 2.4 Key Muscle Groups (Priority Order)

1. **Hip abductors/external rotators** (gluteus medius, gluteus minimus) -- controls frontal plane mechanics, prevents knee valgus
2. **Gluteus maximus** -- primary hip extensor, the "engine" of running
3. **Quadriceps** -- absorbs impact during stance phase
4. **Calf complex** (gastrocnemius and soleus) -- absorbs/redistributes impact forces away from tibia; critical for Achilles and plantar fascia health
5. **Hamstrings** -- eccentric control during swing phase; injury-prone without targeted work
6. **Core** (deep stabilizers) -- pelvic stability during single-leg stance

### 2.5 Evidence-Based Exercise Protocol

**Hip and Core Focus (from Run RCT -- Leppanen 2024):**
- Clamshells with resistance band
- Side-lying hip abduction
- Single-leg glute bridges
- Side planks
- Quadruped hip extensions
- Bird-dogs
- Single-leg deadlifts
- Lateral band walks

**Key additional exercises supported by research:**

| Exercise | Primary target | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **Nordic hamstring curls** | Hamstring eccentric strength | 51% injury reduction (meta-analysis, 8,459 athletes) |
| **Single-leg calf raises** | Soleus/gastrocnemius | Critical for MTSS and Achilles prevention |
| **Bulgarian split squats** | Quads, glutes, single-leg stability | Addresses bilateral asymmetry |
| **Heavy squats (>=70% 1RM)** | Quads, glutes, global | Improves running economy and tendon stiffness |
| **Step-ups with knee drive** | Hip flexors, glutes, stability | Functional running-specific strength |
| **Single-leg Romanian deadlifts** | Hamstrings, glutes, balance | Eccentric hamstring + proprioception |

### 2.6 Sample Weekly Schedule

- **Option A (2x/week):** Full-body sessions on non-consecutive days (e.g., Tuesday and Friday), 20-35 minutes each
- **Option B (3x/week):** Full-body sessions, ideally after easy runs or on separate days from quality running sessions
- Perform strength exercises before running (per Run RCT protocol) or after easy runs
- Never schedule heavy strength work on the same day as a quality running session

## 3. Running Form and Cadence

### 3.1 Cadence Guidelines

| Fact | Detail | Source |
|---|---|---|
| **Origin of "180 spm" rule** | Jack Daniels observed elite runners at 1984 Olympics had >=180 spm | Widely cited coaching literature |
| **Evidence assessment** | "No evidence whatsoever to suggest that 180 has any privileged position as a minimum threshold" -- Bryan Heiderscheit | [Sweat Science Analysis](https://sweatscience.com/the-problem-with-180-strides-per-minute-some-personal-data/) |
| **Optimal range for efficiency** | 165-175 spm where heart rate and oxygen consumption are minimized (PLOS ONE) | [PMC Cadence Study](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12222555/) |
| **Injury association** | Cadence <170 spm associated with increased injury risk, especially stress fractures | [PMC Cadence and Injury](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12440572/) |
| **Speed dependence** | Cadence naturally varies with pace: 160s at jogging pace, 180+ during racing | Biomechanics research |
| **Recommended change** | Increase by 5-10% if overstriding is identified (Heiderscheit protocol) | [E3 Rehab Running Cadence](https://e3rehab.com/running-cadence/) |

### 3.2 Overstriding

- Overstriding = landing with foot too far ahead of center of gravity
- Increases braking forces, ground contact time, and loading rates through joints
- Low cadence increases the probability of overstriding
- **Caveat**: Overstriding has not been confirmed as an independent injury risk factor because there is no agreed-upon "normal" stride distance
- Increasing cadence by 5-10% naturally shortens stride and reduces overstriding without conscious effort to change foot placement

### 3.3 Vertical Oscillation

- Target range: **5-10 cm** for most runners
- Excess vertical oscillation wastes energy (does not contribute to forward motion)
- Cueing runners to reduce vertical oscillation reduces peak vertical ground reaction force
- Higher cadence + lower oscillation together reduce loading rates more than either alone ([PMC Adams et al. 2018](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6088121/))

### 3.4 Ground Contact Time (GCT)

- Shorter GCT is associated with improved running economy in some studies
- Higher cadence naturally reduces GCT
- GCT asymmetry (>2% difference between legs) is strongly related to impaired running economy ([PMC GCT Imbalance Study](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7241633/))
- Useful as a monitoring metric rather than a direct coaching target

### 3.5 Should Form Be Actively Coached?

**The general consensus:**

- The neuromuscular system naturally self-optimizes running form for efficiency over time with training
- Consciously imposing rigid form templates is generally **not** supported as effective for performance
- Two-time Olympian Nicole Sifuentes: extensive form work did not make her faster; just consumed energy and time

**The exception -- injury prevention:**

- Targeted technique changes for specific injury patterns ARE supported:
  - Runners with knee pain: increasing cadence or landing more softly reduced knee pain and improved function at 6-month follow-up
  - Runners with shin splints: cadence increases and gait retraining can reduce tibial loading
  - Runners with IT band syndrome: reducing overstriding reduces lateral knee loading
- Focus on one cue at a time; multiple simultaneous changes are not sustainable

**Practical approach for an AI coach:**
1. Do NOT prescribe blanket form changes to all runners
2. If injury patterns emerge, consider targeted biomechanical cues (increased cadence, reduced overstriding, softer landing)
3. Monitor cadence as a data point; flag if consistently <160 spm at easy pace
4. Allow form to self-optimize through consistent training and strength work

## 4. Load Management

### 4.1 The 10% Rule -- Evidence Assessment

| Claim | Evidence | Source |
|---|---|---|
| "Never increase weekly mileage by >10%" | **Not well-supported.** RCT of 532 novice runners: 10% rule group vs. aggressive group had identical injury rates (~20%) | [JOSPT Buist et al.](https://www.jospt.org/doi/10.2519/jospt.2014.5164) |
| Uninjured runners averaged 22% weekly increases | 2012 Aarhus study: 47 uninjured runners averaged 22.1% weekly increases (double the rule) | [Canadian Running Magazine](https://runningmagazine.ca/sections/training/the-10-per-cent-mileage-rule-isnt-what-you-think-study-warns/) |
| Single-session spikes matter more | 2025 Garmin-RUNSAFE study (5,205 runners, 588K sessions): a single run >110% of longest run in prior month increased overuse injury risk by **64%** | [PMC Garmin-RUNSAFE](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12421110/) |
| Scaling problems | At 5 miles/week, 10% = 0.5 miles (trivial); at 60 miles/week, 10% = 6 miles (potentially too much) | [Marathon Handbook](https://marathonhandbook.com/the-10-rule-new-study-suggests-weve-been-doing-it-wrong-this-whole-time/) |

**Updated recommendation**: Rather than weekly mileage percentage, monitor single-session spikes. Do not exceed 110% of the longest run in the prior 4 weeks. Keep weekly volume increases moderate but individualized based on training history and adaptation.

### 4.2 ACWR Application for Injury Prevention

**Gabbett's Framework (2016):**

| ACWR Zone | Value | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Under-training | <0.8 | Increased injury risk due to deconditioning; athlete is losing fitness |
| Sweet spot | **0.8-1.3** | Lowest injury risk; well-prepared state |
| Danger zone 1 | 1.3-1.5 | Elevated risk; acute load exceeding chronic preparation |
| Danger zone 2 | >=1.5 | High injury risk; acute load greatly exceeds chronic preparation |
| Extreme danger | >=1.8 | Up to 50% higher injury risk |

**Calculation**: ACWR = (week's training load) / (average of past 4 weeks' training load)

**Important caveats:**
- The ACWR has been criticized for mathematical coupling (acute load is double-counted within chronic load) ([PMC Methodological Concerns](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7534945/))
- A 10-month RCT found no injury reduction with ACWR-based load management
- Running-specific validation is limited ([PubMed ACWR Running Comparison](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38291782/))
- Best used as one signal among many, not as a standalone decision tool

**The Training-Injury Prevention Paradox** (Gabbett 2016): High chronic workloads built gradually over time may actually DECREASE injury risk by developing physical resilience. It is poorly managed spikes, not high training loads per se, that cause injuries ([PubMed Gabbett 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26758673/)).

### 4.3 Training Error as Primary Injury Cause

Training errors are considered the main contributor to overuse injury development in runners, encompassing:
- Excessive single-session distance (strongest recent evidence)
- Rapid increase in weekly volume
- Sudden introduction of intensity (speed work, hills)
- Insufficient recovery between hard sessions
- Monotonous training (training monotony >2.0)

### 4.4 Red Flags in Training Load Data

An AI running coach should flag the following patterns:

| Red Flag | Threshold | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Single-run spike** | >110% of longest run in prior month | Alert: high injury risk (64%+ increase per Garmin-RUNSAFE) |
| **ACWR exceeds danger zone** | >1.5 | Warning: reduce acute load or add recovery days |
| **ACWR below sweet spot** | <0.8 | Warning: athlete is deconditioned; gradual re-introduction needed |
| **Training monotony** | >2.0 (mean daily load / SD of daily loads) | Suggest more variation in daily training load |
| **Sudden intensity addition** | First speed session in >2 weeks, or >60% increase in intensity load | Reduce volume of speed work; introduce gradually |
| **Consecutive hard days** | 3+ days without easy/rest day | Flag recovery concern |
| **Elevated resting HR** | >5 bpm above baseline for 3+ consecutive days | Suggest rest or reduced volume; possible overtraining signal |
| **Performance decline** | Declining pace at same HR/RPE for 2+ weeks | Possible overtraining; consider recovery week |
| **Weekly mileage jump** | >30% increase from 4-week rolling average | High risk per Aarhus study (injured group averaged >30% increases) |

## 5. Return-to-Run Protocols

### 5.1 Pre-Running Criteria (Must Meet ALL Before Starting)

1. **Pain-free walking**: 30 minutes continuous without pain and with normalized gait
2. **Resolution of bony tenderness**: No point tenderness over injury site (especially for stress fractures)
3. **Radiological healing**: Required for high-risk stress fractures (femoral neck, anterior tibia, navicular)
4. **Strength benchmarks**: At least 70% of uninvolved side for relevant muscle groups (quadriceps, hamstrings, calf)
5. **Hop testing**: At least 70% of uninvolved side
6. **Contributing factors identified**: Training errors, biomechanical issues, nutritional deficiencies addressed

Sources: [PMC Tibial BSI Scoping Review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11393297/), [PMC Return-to-Sprinting](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7134353/)

### 5.2 The Pain Monitoring Model

The pain monitoring model provides an evidence-based framework for progression decisions:

| Pain Level (0-10 scale) | Interpretation | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **0-2 during activity** | Acceptable / minimal symptoms | Progress to next stage |
| **>2 during activity** | Load tolerance exceeded | Do NOT progress; assess for swelling next morning |
| **No swelling next morning** | Repeat same session | Stay at current level |
| **Swelling present next morning** | Significant overload | Step back 1 session + 1 rest day |
| **0 for 1 hour after activity** | Normal response | Safe to progress |
| **Pain >0 lasting >1 hour after** | Excessive load | Step back; do not progress |

Source: [PMC ACL RTR Pain Algorithm](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9387380/)

The "24-hour rule": Mild soreness that resolves within 24 hours is acceptable. Symptoms persisting beyond 24 hours indicate the training load was excessive.

### 5.3 Walk-Run Progression Protocol

**Phase 1: Walk-Run Intervals (Weeks 1-3)**

| Week | Walk:Run ratio | Total session time | Sessions/week |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 4 min walk : 1 min run | 25-30 min | 3 |
| 2 | 3 min walk : 2 min run | 25-30 min | 3 |
| 3 | 2 min walk : 3 min run | 30 min | 3-4 |

**Phase 2: Run-Walk Transition (Weeks 4-6)**

| Week | Walk:Run ratio | Total session time | Sessions/week |
|---|---|---|---|
| 4 | 1 min walk : 4 min run | 30 min | 3-4 |
| 5 | 1 min walk : 9 min run | 30 min | 3-4 |
| 6 | Continuous easy running | 20-30 min | 4 |

**Phase 3: Volume Rebuild (Weeks 7-12+)**
- Increase duration by 10-15% per week
- Progress distance before speed
- Begin on predictable surfaces (treadmill, track) before varied terrain
- Do not add intensity until reaching 75-80% of pre-injury weekly mileage

Each session starts and ends with 5 minutes of walking. Run at self-selected easy/jogging pace (8-10 km/h). Apply the pain monitoring model at every session.

Sources: [Ohio State Wexner Return to Running Guideline](https://wexnermedical.osu.edu/-/media/files/wexnermedical/patient-care/healthcare-services/sports-medicine/education/medical-professionals/other/basic-return-to-running-guideline.pdf), [RunnersConnect RTR Protocol](https://runnersconnect.net/running-injury-return/)

### 5.4 Criteria-Based vs. Time-Based Return

**Criteria-based approach is strongly preferred:**
- Runners who follow structured, criteria-based protocols have significantly lower re-injury rates
- Time-based protocols do not account for individual healing rates, strength deficits, or biomechanical issues
- Pain relief does NOT equal tissue healing -- premature return based solely on time is the leading cause of re-injury

**Return-to-competition benchmarks:**
- Complete 75-80% of pre-injury weekly mileage without symptoms for 2-3 consecutive weeks
- Pass functional strength and hop tests at >=90% of uninvolved side
- No pain during or after sport-specific activities for 2 consecutive weeks

### 5.5 Injury-Specific Return Guidelines

| Injury | Typical off-running period | Return-to-run onset | Full training |
|---|---|---|---|
| PFPS | 1-4 weeks (modified running may continue) | When pain-free stairs and squats | 4-8 weeks |
| ITBS | 2-6 weeks | When pain-free walking 30 min | 6-12 weeks |
| MTSS | 2-6 weeks | When pain-free walking + no tibial tenderness | 8-16 weeks |
| Plantar fasciitis | 0-4 weeks (modified running may continue) | When first-step pain resolves | 3-6 months |
| Achilles tendinopathy | 2-8 weeks (modified loading) | When morning stiffness resolves + eccentric exercises pain-free | 3-6 months |
| Low-risk stress fracture | 6-8 weeks | Pain-free walking + no point tenderness | 12-14 weeks from diagnosis |
| High-risk stress fracture | 8-16 weeks | Pain-free walking + imaging confirmation of healing | 16-24 weeks |
| Hamstring strain (Grade 1) | 1-2 weeks | Pain-free walking + 70% strength | 3-6 weeks |
| Hamstring strain (Grade 2) | 4-6 weeks | 70% strength + pain-free eccentric loading | 8-12 weeks |

## 6. Mobility and Flexibility

### 6.1 Static vs. Dynamic Stretching

| Timing | Type | Evidence | Recommendation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pre-run** | Dynamic stretching | Static stretching reduces muscle strength by up to 9% for 60 minutes; reduces contraction speed by 8% and rate of force development by 5% | Use dynamic stretching only: leg swings, walking lunges, high knees, butt kicks, A-skips |
| **Post-run** | Static stretching | Muscles are warm; stretching aids blood flow and lactic acid removal; helps prevent stiffness | Hold stretches 20-30 seconds per muscle group; target calves, hamstrings, hip flexors, quads |
| **Separate sessions** | Static stretching | Some evidence that "stiffer" muscle-tendon units improve running economy via elastic energy return | Optional; may improve flexibility but excessive flexibility is not necessarily beneficial for runners |

Sources: [PMC Stretching and Running Economy](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8391672/), [Yale Medicine Pre-Run Stretching](https://www.yalemedicine.org/news/how-to-stretch-before-run), [Cleveland Clinic Dynamic vs Static](https://health.clevelandclinic.org/dynamic-stretching-vs-static-stretching)

**The flexibility paradox for runners**: Multiple studies suggest that stiffer runners are actually more economical than more flexible runners, likely due to greater elastic energy storage and return during the stretch-shortening cycle. This does NOT mean runners should avoid all stretching, but it does mean excessive flexibility is not a goal.

### 6.2 Foam Rolling

| Outcome | Evidence | Effect Size |
|---|---|---|
| **Range of motion** | Strong evidence for improvement | Large effect (d = 0.76); effective for hamstrings and quadriceps ROM; NOT effective for ankle dorsiflexion via triceps surae rolling |
| **Recovery** | Moderate evidence | May mitigate declines in strength, power after intense exercise; reduces perceived pain |
| **Performance** | Trivial/no evidence for direct benefit | No meaningful acute performance improvement |
| **Optimal protocol** | No consensus | 30 seconds to 2 minutes per muscle group; interventions >4 weeks show greater ROM improvements than <=4 weeks |

Sources: [PMC Foam Rolling Meta-Analysis](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6465761/), [PMC Foam Rolling ROM Review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9474417/), [PMC Foam Rolling Training Effects](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9517147/)

**Practical guidance**: Foam rolling is a reasonable recovery tool and pre-activity warm-up aid for ROM, but should not replace strength training or targeted mobility work. It does not appear to cause harm.

### 6.3 Hip Mobility

- Excessive hip adduction and internal rotation during running are associated with multiple injuries (PFPS, ITBS, tibial stress fractures)
- Weakness in hip abductors and external rotators contributes to dynamic knee valgus
- The Run RCT (2024) demonstrated that hip-focused exercise programs reduce running injuries by 39%
- Runners with stress fractures show greater peak hip adduction and rearfoot eversion during stance phase
- Hip mobility and strength should be assessed together, as mobility without motor control is insufficient

### 6.4 Ankle Dorsiflexion

| Finding | Evidence |
|---|---|
| Restricted dorsiflexion linked to compensatory movement patterns | Reduced hip and knee flexion, increased quadriceps loading, "stiff-legged" landing ([PMC Taylor et al.](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9112706/)) |
| Association with dynamic knee valgus | Meta-analysis: SMD -0.65 (95% CI -0.88 to -0.41) comparing reduced dorsiflexion and knee valgus groups |
| Linked injuries | Plantar fasciitis, shin splints, knee pain, ACL injuries, hamstring strains |
| Foot-strike relationship | Midfoot strikers have significantly greater dorsiflexion than rearfoot strikers |
| Assessment method | Weight-bearing lunge test (WBLT); measure distance of knee past toes or degrees of tibial angle |
| Normal range | >=35 degrees or >=10 cm in WBLT |

Source: [Frontiers Ankle Dorsiflexion Review](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/sports-and-active-living/articles/10.3389/fspor.2025.1677383/full), [Electronic Journal of General Medicine](https://www.ejgm.co.uk/article/role-of-ankle-dorsiflexion-in-sports-performance-and-injury-risk-a-narrative-review-13412)

**Practical approach**: Screen ankle dorsiflexion in all runners. If restricted, prescribe targeted calf stretching, ankle mobilization exercises, and weight-bearing dorsiflexion drills. Address both joint mobility restrictions and tissue flexibility limitations.

## 7. Risk Factors

### 7.1 Risk Factor Hierarchy (by Evidence Strength)

| Risk Factor | Strength of Evidence | Magnitude |
|---|---|---|
| **Previous injury (within 12 months)** | Strong -- consistently the strongest predictor across all reviews | 2x risk (HR 2.0+) ([JOSPT Prospective Study](https://www.jospt.org/doi/10.2519/jospt.2021.9673)) |
| **Training errors / load spikes** | Strong | 64-128% increased risk depending on spike magnitude ([PMC Garmin-RUNSAFE](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12421110/)) |
| **Running experience <5 years** | Moderate | Higher risk in novice runners |
| **Weekly mileage >30-64 km** | Moderate (conflicting) | HR 2.88-3.28 at higher thresholds, but some studies show no effect |
| **Higher running speed** | Moderate | Consistent association |
| **Running >3 times/week** | Low-moderate | Identified in some prospective studies |
| **BMI >26 (or >30 for novices)** | Low-moderate (conflicting) | OR 1.05 per BMI unit; but some studies show >26 as protective in men |
| **Running surface** | Weak | No significant effect in large study of 4,358 runners; body self-adjusts loading ([Springer RISC Study](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s40798-023-00589-1)) |

### 7.2 Previous Injury History

- **The single most consistent and strongest predictor of future running injury**
- Runners with prior injury in the last 12 months are **twice as likely** to sustain a new running-related injury ([JOSPT 2021](https://www.jospt.org/doi/10.2519/jospt.2021.9673))
- 20-70% of running injuries recur
- Knowledge of injury history should increase monitoring sensitivity for biomechanical risk factors related to past injuries
- An AI coach should weight prior injury history heavily when assessing risk and programming training

### 7.3 Weekly Mileage Thresholds

- Evidence is conflicting for absolute mileage thresholds
- Three high-quality studies identified elevated risk at:
  - >30 km/week (HR = 3.28)
  - >64 km/week (adjusted RR = 2.88)
  - 60-70 km/week (95-100% increased risk vs. 40-50 km/week)
- Changes in mileage likely matter more than absolute values
- The relationship is U-shaped: very low mileage (undertraining) may also increase risk due to deconditioning

Sources: [PMC Risk Factor Systematic Review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7856562/), [PMC Training Parameters Review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9528699/)

### 7.4 BMI

- Higher BMI is associated with increased RRI risk: OR 1.05 (95% CI 1.02-1.09) per unit increase
- Novice runners with BMI >30: high risk
- BMI >26: 3x risk in some studies, but **protective** in male recreational runners in others
- BMI <19: increased risk of stress fractures
- Higher BMI is specifically associated with MTSS; lower BMI with stress fractures
- BMI is an imperfect metric -- does not distinguish fat mass from lean muscle mass
- Sex-specific BMI effects: higher BMI = risk factor for back injuries in women; lower BMI = risk factor for foot injuries in men

Sources: [PLOS ONE Sex Differences Review](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0114937), [ResearchGate BMI Meta-Analysis](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/391072749)

### 7.5 Running Surface

- The body's "muscle tuning" mechanism adjusts leg stiffness to maintain similar overall impact across surfaces
- A large study of 4,358 runners found no significant influence of surface type on injury risk
- However, trail running shows **higher injury rates** (10.7-19.6 per 1,000 hours) vs. road running (2.5-5.8 per 1,000 hours), likely due to longer distances and traumatic injuries (ankle sprains)
- Treadmill running: 14% greater Achilles tendon stress than overground
- **Recommendation**: Vary surfaces to distribute loading; no single surface is clearly superior or inferior

Sources: [Outside Magazine Surface Study](https://www.outsideonline.com/health/running/training-advice/science/how-running-surface-and-speed-influence-injury-risk/), [PMC Trail Running Injury Review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8811510/)

### 7.6 Shoe Type and Rotation

**Minimalist vs. Maximalist:**

| Shoe Type | Evidence |
|---|---|
| **Minimalist** | May contribute to short-term foot/lower leg pain; increased risk for runners >71.4 kg body mass (HR 2.0 at 85.7 kg); potential long-term plantar-flexor strength gains at 20+ weeks |
| **Maximalist** | Does not reduce vertical ground reaction forces, but appears less likely to cause running-related pain; may provide some protective value from thick midsole |
| **Conventional** | Standard recommendation; no strong evidence for any specific shoe type preventing injuries |

**Shoe Rotation:**

- Landmark study (n=264, 22 weeks): parallel use of >1 pair = **39% reduced injury risk** (HR 0.614) ([PubMed Shoe Rotation Study](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24286345/))
- Proposed mechanism: load variation across different midsole geometries reduces repetitive strain on specific tissues
- Minimum viable: 2 complementary pairs, alternating with 24-48 hours between uses of same shoe
- Optimal: 3-4 distinct models for greatest loading variation
- **Caveat**: Not an RCT; may reflect selection bias (health-conscious runners rotate shoes)

**Shoe lifespan**: Replace running shoes after 300-500 miles; sooner if running primarily on hard surfaces.

### 7.7 Age and Sex Differences

**Sex differences:**
- Females: mean injury incidence 39.7% vs. males 34.3%
- Females: greatest prevalence at calf/lower leg; more stress fractures
- Males: greatest incidence at hip/pelvis/upper leg
- Risk factors differ by sex: BMI is a stronger predictor in females; running experience is a stronger predictor in males
- Female athletes: must screen for RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport); menstrual dysfunction is a significant stress fracture risk factor

**Age:**
- Moderate quality evidence that age is a risk factor, but conflicting findings
- Older age may be protective in males (likely due to experience and wisdom in training)
- Age younger than 31 is protective in females
- Short-distance runners aged 45-65 are at higher risk
- Age-related reduction in tissue elasticity increases risk for tendinopathies

Sources: [PLOS ONE Sex Differences](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0114937), [PMC Overuse Injury Risk Factors](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7856562/)
